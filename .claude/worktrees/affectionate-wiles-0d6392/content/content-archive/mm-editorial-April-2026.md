## A Kitchen Reborn: One Chef’s Mission to Restore Fine Dining at The Lodge — and Train Who Comes Next
**By Drew Gaines**  
**Page 1**

Step inside the kitchen at 1899 at the Lodge at Cloudcroft, where Executive Chef Richard Lepree and his team are writing the next chapter for one of the Southwest’s most storied hotels.

In this short film, Drew Gaines takes you into the kitchen as the team prepares a tasting dinner for a group of Cloudcroft locals.

You’ll hear Lepree explain the difference between a cook and a chef using nothing but a tomato. You’ll watch Sous chef Caelan Bickel, who grew up on green chili and Texas food, describe the adventure of learning a whole new way to think about ingredients. And you’ll see a kitchen where everyone has a voice — where the newest menu is the first one the entire team helped create.

Lepree, a 34-year veteran of the culinary business who trained in Michelin kitchens, leads a young brigade of Cloudcroft-grown chefs who share his passion for cooking from scratch. Chef de partie Bentley McNab sears pineapple for the shrimp salad. Pastry chef Jewlyn Palmer keeps the line moving through what she cheerfully calls “a chaotic dance.”

Together, they’ve built seasonal menus: most recently featuring ahi ceviche, prime New York strips, elk backstrap and mussels over handmade pasta — all prepared fresh, locally sourced when the season allows, and made with care.

Lepree calls The Lodge his last kitchen, and he means it as a compliment. After decades of cooking across two countries, he found the place where he wants to be. Now he’s pouring that experience into a team he hopes will carry it forward long after he steps away from the line.

Pull up a chair and meet the crew. Scan the QR code in the print issue to watch the video, or visit Cloudcroft Reader for the film.

## Legacy of Liberty Air Show at Holloman AFB
**Page 2**

West of Alamogordo, Holloman Air Force Base will host its free, two-day Legacy of Liberty Air Show on April 18–19, with gates opening at 8 a.m. and the event running until 5 p.m. both days.

The air show is open to the general public, with access through the base’s main gates and parking available directly on the airfield.

“People don’t need a special base pass. Entry and parking is free,” says base Public Affairs Media Chief Denise Ottaviano.

The 49th Wing — home to the Air Force’s largest F-16 Fighting Falcon pilot training unit and MQ-9 Reaper aircrew formal training unit — is hosting the show, with mission partners across Team Holloman also on hand to showcase their roles in national defense.

Headlining the flying program is the Patriots Jet Team. The largest civilian-owned aerobatic jet team in the western hemisphere, the Patriots are made up of highly experienced aviators — some of whom previously flew with the U.S. Air Force Thunderbirds and U.S. Navy Blue Angels — and will perform in six Aero L-39 Albatros trainer jets.

They’ll be joined by a roster of performers from around the country and beyond, including Australian aerobatic pilot Aarron Deliu, Holloman’s own F-16s in a flight demonstration, and two parachute teams: the U.S. Air Force Academy Wings of Blue and the U.S. Army Golden Knights.

On the ground, attendees can browse static displays spanning heritage warplanes through present-day F-35s, plus exotic cars. The 704th Test Group and 635th Materiel Maintenance Group will square off in a weapons loading competition, racing to load inert bombs onto aircraft in front of the crowd.

“That will give people some insight into what we do here at the 49th Wing to help defend our country,” Ottaviano said.

A STEM zone with hands-on activities and bouncy castles, a kid zone, food vendors, and live music round out the weekend’s offerings.

Either day, the flight line is fully exposed — April in southern New Mexico means sunshine. Patrons are encouraged to bring a folding chair, sunscreen, a hat, and a personal umbrella for shade.

Shade structures are not permitted. Water refill stations will be on the grounds; bring a refillable water bottle. Food, drinks, and concessions will be available for purchase.

Strollers and wagons are welcome. Security checkpoints will be in place — clear bags are strongly recommended to speed up entry, and no weapons of any kind, pocket knives, or drugs including cannabis are permitted.

“We just can’t wait to open our doors to the public so they can see the spectacular aerial display we have planned and come out and meet our airmen,” Ottaviano said.

For more information and updates, visit holloman.af.mil.

## Fire Season 2026: What Cloudcroft Residents Need to Know
**Pages 6–7**

Joe Kimble, the Sacramento Ranger District’s Suppression Fire Management Officer, covers fire season conditions, resources, alerts, fuel projects, and what residents can do.

Joe Kimble took over as Suppression FMO for the Sacramento Ranger District of the Lincoln National Forest earlier this year. He previously served as FMO on the Guadalupe Ranger District and has worked in wildland fire for more than two decades, starting on an engine crew on the Apache-Sitgreaves in Arizona.

Kimble and his family have lived in Cloudcroft for 13 years, where he coaches football as a volunteer with Cloudcroft Schools.

The article reports that conditions in the Sacramento Mountains are somewhat improved over 2025, but not enough to lower the district’s alert posture.

“Last year was probably a little bit more droughty than what we are this year. Still a lot of fine, dead, dry fuels out there. But I would say we’re definitely not out of the woods,” he said.

“We haven’t been getting the snow packs that we’ve been normally getting, historically, for several years now,” Kimble said. “I wouldn’t say it’s super scary, but we’re definitely paying attention with a really close eye.”

Kimble identified the main factors driving fire risk: fuel moisture, dead fuel loads, weather, and human activity.

Prevention crews go out weekly to pull fuel samples — measuring moisture in grasses, brush, downed wood, and living trees — to track whether conditions are improving or deteriorating. Relative humidity, incoming wind events, and regional trends all feed into the district’s preparedness decisions.

“A good majority of our fires are human-caused — particularly in this area,” Kimble said.

When asked if forest closures were likely, Kimble said, “My answer is always, I’ll tell you next fall.” He said the district implements Stage 1 and Stage 2 fire restrictions before resorting to full closures. Full closure is a last resort.

On March 16, just days after the interview, the district moved to “high” fire danger.

### Resources and staffing

The Sacramento Ranger District fields two Type 6 and two Type 3 engines in Cloudcroft, plus two additional engines in Mayhill.

The full Lincoln National Forest fields eight engines and two hotshot crews, the Sac Hotshots and Smokey Bear Hotshots on the Smokey Bear Ranger District.

Kimble said seasonal firefighters are currently onboarding, working through two weeks of required critical training before the season starts. Additional resources are brought in based on fuel moisture, weather forecasts, and regional preparedness levels.

He said fire staffing has been largely insulated from recent federal layoffs or funding freezes, though other entities have not.

The district has one law enforcement officer for the Sacramento Ranger District. During restrictions, prevention personnel and Forest Protection Officers handle much of the patrol work, with extra patrol capacity added during holiday weekends and periods of elevated fire danger.

Kimble said the approach is education first: “Some people just honestly don’t know. We really lean toward educating the public ... before we just start writing tickets.”

### Partnerships and coordination

Kimble said relationships with local volunteer departments have improved significantly. In the wildland-urban interface around Cloudcroft, volunteer departments are often first on scene.

“They beat us to the fires a lot of times, because they’re there in those areas,” Kimble said. Once Forest Service crews arrive, operations shift into unified command when needed.

### Fuel reduction projects

Several fuel-reduction and prescribed-burn projects are planned or underway.

The largest project in the district covers up to 140,000 acres in the southern Sacramento Mountains. The Lincoln National Forest completed its final Environmental Impact Statement in December 2024, opening a 30-day public objection period. Kimble described the project as “coming online soon.”

It focuses on mixed conifer and ponderosa pine forests affected by insect infestation, disease, and drought-driven tree mortality. Restoration tools include mechanical thinning and prescribed fire. Implementation is expected to span 10 to 20 years.

A separate fuels treatment project covering roughly 3,000 acres around Cloudcroft’s south and western boundaries is in development. Wildlife and botany surveys were completed in 2024, with heritage surveys and fuels work to follow. The project must still undergo National Environmental Policy Act review.

Kimble said that from project conception to environmental clearance, implementation can take three to four years.

He also addressed large wood chips left by mastication projects, saying they decompose faster than logs and can help buy time before a broadcast burn.

### Alerts and what residents should know

Otero County and Lincoln County have implemented Genasys Protect, formerly Zonehaven, a zone-based emergency notification platform. When an evacuation order is issued for a zone, alerts go to any phone within that geofenced area, including visitors not registered with the county.

Kimble pointed readers to three sources for real-time fire information:
- the Lincoln National Forest social media page
- InciWeb
- the National Weather Service El Paso

On home hardening, Kimble emphasized that residents need to do their part.

“The biggest message I would like to see get out there is the public doing their part — from respecting Forest Service grounds and recreating responsibly, but also with their private property,” he said.

“Because I, as a fire management officer, have a hard time with sending my guys in to do the work that should have already been done. They’re putting their lives on the line. People have to do their own home hardening and be prepared for themselves. That’s the biggest message. People have got to do their due diligence.”

## Village Update: Council Hires Familiar Face for Second Police Officer. Plus, Higher Trash Rates and a Fiscal Roadmap
**Pages 7–8**

The Cloudcroft Village Council met on Tuesday, March 17, to cover a wide agenda. Trustees unanimously voted to fill a police department vacancy, increase garbage billing rates that had not been updated since 2023, and put a state-required financial compliance plan on the record.

### Police Department: Testa returns

The council unanimously approved hiring Mike Testa as a police officer at a starting salary of $70,720.

Testa served as the village’s chief of police until May 2024, when he resigned alongside Deputy Chief David Sanchez and Officer Calib Bruce during a dispute over a proposed Panama schedule for a four-person force. He later went to work as a logger with Ellinger Logging.

Chief Roger Schoolcraft, who had also resigned in December 2024 before returning under Mayor Dusty Wiley’s administration, told the council he had reviewed no fewer than 20 applicants and selected only Testa.

Schoolcraft said Testa brings “a lot of character to the badge” and has family and businesses rooted in the village.

The plan is for Schoolcraft and Testa to work a split schedule to provide seven-day coverage. Testa can return to active duty after completing basic firearms qualifications.

Testa told the Reader he was happy and proud to return and serve the community, and excited to return under the new administration. His focus, he said, is to “keep people safe.”

Schoolcraft noted that the federal Safe Schools grant, which would fund half the salary of a desired school resource officer, remains frozen.

### Water report

Public Works Supervisor J.J. Carrizal reported the village was running at roughly 5% water loss for February, well below the national average of 16%.

The village produced about 4.3 million gallons and sold 4.1 million.

Carrizal said the figure marked a meaningful improvement from earlier this year, when the village was dealing with what he called “the big one” — a major leak on Columbine Boulevard that had been losing an estimated 2.5 million gallons per month before it was found and repaired in early February.

### Garbage collection rates

The council approved two waste-collection rate adjustments.

The first was a contractually allowed 2.4% increase, raising the collection haul rate from $17.36 to $17.88, effective July 1 through the Southwest Disposal contract.

The second addressed CPI-based rate increases approved in July 2024 and July 2025 that were never entered into the billing software. Village Clerk Jini Turri said the village had been absorbing the difference from the general fund.

Trustee Matt Willett asked whether residents would be billed retroactively. Turri said no. The approved 7% increase was expected to take effect with the May billing cycle once the software was updated.

### Fiscal compliance resolution

The council approved Resolution 2026-11, formally addressing a corrective action plan required by the state Department of Finance and Administration.

The FY2024 independent audit had identified significant deficiencies in bank reconciliations, accounts payable, segregation of duties, capital asset records, and accounting documentation.

Turri said the work is now falling primarily on her shoulders, with support from Finance Director Sylvia Hall and outside contractors Karen Gutierrez, Judi Starkovich, and DFA budget analyst Bryce Brock.

She said she eventually plans to request a deputy clerk, but not yet. Her immediate goal is to get the books current so fiscal year 2026–27 “looks a lot better.”

### Fire restrictions and other actions

The Lincoln National Forest recently raised its fire danger rating to “High” across all three ranger districts, effective March 16.

The council authorized Mayor Wiley to implement or adjust fire restrictions during fire season without requiring a council vote each time. Fire Chief Erich Wuersching said he coordinates closely with the Forest Service, State Forestry, Otero County, and neighboring communities so restrictions can be aligned when needed.

Other unanimously approved actions included:
- a preliminary engineering report contract with CDM Smith for a wastewater treatment plant master plan, funded by an existing grant
- a Clean and Beautiful grant application submitted by local artist and Burro Avenue business owner Samantha Odom
- a special use permit for Andy Anderson to sell chainsaw carvings near Cloud 9000 Coffee and Fernando’s Burritos
- authorization to submit an NMDOT Transportation Project Fund application for paving roads
- a variance for a property at 1521 Rainmaker consistent with a setback granted under a prior owner in 2015

## Taking Aim: Cloudcroft Schools Launch New Archery Program
**Page 9**

In the mountains of southern New Mexico, Cloudcroft Middle School students are taking aim at something new.

New Mexico Department of Game and Fish Officer Anthony Chavez and Cloudcroft Police Chief Roger Schoolcraft recently led the school’s physical education students through their first lesson in the National Archery in the Schools Program, or NASP.

Together, they walked students through safety rules, whistle commands, and the program’s eleven foundational steps of proper archery form.

“Not everybody plays basketball. Not everybody plays softball. This is definitely a sport, whether you are in sports or not,” Chavez told students. “It just gives you another opportunity and something to look forward to in the future.”

### Target curriculum

Physical education coach Tami Alexander, herself a bow hunter, is leading the effort inside the school. She is one of five Cloudcroft staff members pursuing the eight-hour Basic Archery Instructor certification required by the New Mexico Department of Game and Fish before a school can officially launch the program.

Others interested in continuing the program are teachers Bryan Casinger, Terri Snoddy, Brent Jewell, and Alana Blaine.

Students must have a parent or guardian waiver on file before taking part.

NASP is a nationally recognized nonprofit curriculum active in more than a hundred New Mexico public schools and sponsored statewide by the Game and Fish Department.

Principal Nolene Adams said the school came to the program mid-cycle this year, which shaped the rollout.

“We plan to continue offering the program through our PE class and begin preparing students for competition next year, as we learned about the competition timeline too late this year,” Adams said.

“The NASP Program is designed for 4th–12th grade,” Adams said, “but to get started, we are currently working with 7th graders to understand the program and how it fits with our school.”

Safety, Chavez said, is foundational. “There have been no accidents to this day that have ever happened in the National Archery in the Schools program. So let’s keep it that way.”

## Peak Performance
**By Paula Finch**  
**Page 9**

Each month, this column follows sports seasons at Cloudcroft High School, highlighting the athletes who represent the school with strength, grit, and pride.

This month’s column focused on powerlifting and track and field.

Powerlifting, Finch wrote, has become a place where Cloudcroft athletes set goals, push themselves, and build confidence. On March 13, Cloudcroft hosted its home powerlifting invitational.

The girls team placed in four different weight classes, led by Gena Davis in the 114-pound class with a 475-pound total. McKenzie Ciulla, Callie Peterson, and Sophia Trejo also finished in the top three in their divisions.

On the boys side, Christopher Yousif won the 132-pound class with a 755-pound total. Levi Edwards added a 760-pound total and a runner-up finish at 198. Angelo Harrell and Nathan Brokaw also medaled, and Colton Jazdzewski posted an outstanding lift in the 181-pound class.

The column then turned to track and field, where CHS athletes recently traveled to Carlsbad to open the season. Sophomore Cassia Moore had already qualified in triple jump.

Finch wrote that her goal is not just to report scores, but to show the dedication, teamwork, and resilience behind Cloudcroft sports.

## Chamber of Cloudcroft 2026 Update
**Pages 9–10**

Cloudcroft’s Chamber has a full 2026 lineup on the books, plus a new Labor Day weekend “Hoopla” built around food trucks and a car show.

Manager Madison Garner shared what the group has in the works for 2026.

### Year at a glance

- **Mayfair Juried Art Show:** May 23–24 at Zenith Park. Art, vendors, and food trucks.
- **BeerFest:** June 13–14 at Zenith Park. Live music both days and family-friendly programming.
- **Christmas in July:** July 11–12 at Zenith Park.
- **Art & Wine in the Cool Pines:** August 7–9 at the Sacramento Mountains Museum and Pioneer Village.

### Fourth of July plans

For the summer’s 250th-anniversary Fourth of July celebration, the chamber will assist the Village of Cloudcroft, Trustee and library aide Gail McCoy, and other partners as they plan a parade, pie-baking contest, and village-wide activities.
