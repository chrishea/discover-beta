# The Lodge at Cloudcroft: Booking Guide

## 1. Executive summary

The Lodge is a historic destination stay, not a standard mountain hotel. Its strongest assets are setting, history, dining, golf, and atmosphere. Its likely tradeoffs are the usual ones that come with older properties: room variation, less uniformity, stairs in some room categories, and the need to verify details that may matter more here than at a chain hotel. If you want a memorable Cloudcroft weekend built around place and character, it looks worth serious consideration. If you want modern hotel predictability above all else, The Grand Cloudcroft Hotel or a cabin rental may be a safer fit.

## 3. What the hotel appears to be

The Lodge appears to be a historic mountain inn-resort hybrid where the main value is atmosphere. The official site sells individuality, antique furnishings, fireplaces, chandeliers, views, and history rather than brand-standard uniformity. Local reporting reinforces that identity and describes the property as one of the centerpieces of Cloudcroft’s civic and tourism life.

In practical terms, it looks best for:
- couples
- golfers
- wedding guests
- history-minded travelers
- weekenders who want to build a stay around the property itself

It looks less ideal for:
- travelers who want chain-level consistency
- guests with strict accessibility needs
- travelers who want a straightforward modern hotel without historic quirks

## 4. Rooms and accommodations

What is confirmed from the current official rooms page:
- the property offers Platinum Rooms
- Victorian Rooms
- Retreat Suites
- a Governor’s Suite

What the official page says about these categories:
- **Platinum Rooms** are newly renovated in an American Craftsman style and include heated bathroom tile floor, flat-screen TV, pillow-top mattress, in-room coffee, microwave, and mini refrigerator.
- **Victorian Rooms** are on the third floor, have chandeliers, padded walls, pillow-top mattress, in-room coffee, microwave, mini refrigerator, and views; they are stairs only.
- **Retreat Suites** are in a private cabin across the parking lot from the main hotel and include four suites plus a shared common area.
- The **Governor’s Suite** includes a fireplace, card table, freestanding tub, heated tile floor, in-room coffee, microwave, and mini refrigerator.

What is likely but should still be treated carefully:
- room sizes and layouts likely vary a lot because the official site says no two rooms are alike and uses antique-furnishings language rather than standardized layouts.
- climate control appears to include air conditioning on at least some rooms according to recent booking-platform pages, but the official rooms page does not spell that out room by room. Appears current; less than 95% certain for every category.

Likely strengths:
- character
- views
- special-room appeal
- some renovated room categories with stronger in-room amenities

Likely drawbacks:
- historic properties can have more room-to-room variability
- some rooms are stair-access only
- historic charm may come with smaller bathrooms, more uneven layout logic, or more noise sensitivity, though I cannot verify those as hard facts. Less than 95% certain; pattern inference only.

## 5. Dining and drinking

Current official dining setup:
- **1899** is the main restaurant. The dining page says it is open Wednesday–Saturday, 5:00 PM–8:00 PM.
- **St. Andrew’s Lounge** is also current on the same page and is open Wednesday–Saturday, 12:00 PM–8:00 PM.
- **Breakfast at The Lodge** is described as included for staying guests and served Saturday and Sunday, 8:00 AM–11:00 AM. Appears current; should be checked directly at booking.

Dining matters here. Local reporting and the current official page both show the hotel actively investing in its food-and-beverage identity. The dining page explicitly frames 1899 as the reimagined successor to Rebecca’s, and a Cloudcroft Reader feature ties Executive Chef Richard Lepree to the current culinary direction.

Bottom line: dining is not just an add-on here. It is one of the strongest reasons to stay on property.

## 6. Golf, grounds, and setting

Golf is a core part of the property’s identity, not a minor amenity. The hotel site, Chamber page, tourism sources, and social pages all present golf as part of the full Lodge experience. The course appears current for 2026, and Lodge social pages say it is open for the season.

What that adds for a guest:

- grounds and views beyond the hotel building itself
- a natural half-day activity without leaving property
- more value for couples, golfers, and wedding or retreat guests

Beyond golf, current booking-platform pages list:

Appears current; should be checked directly.

The setting is one of the clearest strengths. The official site and official history both emphasize the 9,000-foot elevation and mountain views.

## 7. History and significance

The strongest official timeline is:
- resort project established in 1899
- the original log-constructed Lodge reached completion in 1901
- the original Lodge burned in 1909
- the rebuilt Lodge reopened on its current site in 1911

The property matters because it is not just another old hotel. It is one of the defining structures in Cloudcroft’s history and identity. Cloudcroft Reader’s 2024 feature describes it as the town’s most architecturally significant structure, key venue, and emotional anchor. That is local editorial judgment, but it is grounded in the property’s long civic role.

More recent history that matters to bookers:
- owner Joe Duncan told Cloudcroft Reader in 2024 that he and Lanna Duncan had owned and operated the property since 2015 and had invested in restoring it. The Lodge’s own official history page also says New Mexico Adventures, LLC, owned by Lanna and Joe Duncan, entered ownership in March 2015.

## 8. Guest experience patterns

Hard facts and review-pattern impressions need to stay separate.

### Hard facts
The property is historic, has on-site dining, golf, pool/hot tub, and free parking/Wi‑Fi on current booking-platform pages. Some room categories are more updated and better equipped than others.

### Repeated recent review patterns
Across Booking, Hotels.com, and TripAdvisor snippets, the repeated positives are:
- strong setting and views
- charm / atmosphere
- helpful front-desk or staff mentions
- the appeal of the historic property itself

The repeated cautions are:
- upkeep and cleanliness are not praised uniformly
- some rooms or public areas may feel more worn than others
- value can depend heavily on whether you buy into the historic-atmosphere tradeoff

That is fairly typical for older destination hotels: the magic is real, but uniformity is not guaranteed.

## 9. What travelers should verify before booking

Ask directly about:
- which room category you are booking
- whether that specific room has air conditioning
- stairs vs easier access
- pet-friendly-room availability
- exact breakfast terms for your rate
- current pool and hot tub availability
- current dining days and whether 1899 needs reservations
- current golf season and tee-time availability
- cancellation rules for your exact travel dates, especially holidays and event weekends

If accessibility matters, ask pointed questions. The property has no elevator per Hotels.com, and at least one room category is explicitly stairs-only.
