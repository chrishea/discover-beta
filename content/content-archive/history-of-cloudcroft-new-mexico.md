# History of Cloudcroft, New Mexico

## 1. Executive summary

Cloudcroft exists because railroad men looking for timber found something else they could sell: cool air, mountain scenery, and relief from the heat below.

In the late 1890s, Charles Bishop Eddy and his railroad interests were building north from El Paso and needed timber for ties, trestles, and expansion. Survey crews sent into the Sacramento Mountains found dense timber, but they also found a high, forested summit that could work as a resort. That combination — extractive economy plus tourism economy — shaped Cloudcroft from the start.

The town was not an old ranching village that slowly became a resort. It was planned as a mountain destination tied to the railroad. The Pavilion opened first in 1899, the rail line reached the summit in early 1900, and The Lodge became the town’s enduring symbol. Logging, excursion travel, and resort life all grew together.

Cloudcroft’s history is therefore a layered one. It is a railroad town, a timber town, a summer resort, a civic village, and now a tourism-based mountain community inside the orbit of Lincoln National Forest. Fires, the end of rail service, the rise of the automobile, recurring water stress, and periodic reinvention all changed the place. But the basic logic remains visible: Cloudcroft still depends on altitude, scenery, and the idea of escape.

## 2. Why Cloudcroft exists

Cloudcroft was created because the railroad pushed into the Sacramento Mountains and then realized the summit could be marketed as a destination, not just logged as a resource.

The broader background begins with Charles Bishop Eddy. A National Register historical context study on the railroad logging era says Eddy shifted his attention to the Sacramento Mountains and railroads in the 1890s. Once the El Paso and Northeastern line began developing, a timber shortage for ties and railroad construction helped push the company toward a mountain branch line. That line became the Alamogordo and Sacramento Mountain Railway. The same National Register study says the railroad logging era laid the foundations for both commercial and tourist industries in the Sacramento Mountains, and that towns such as Alamogordo and Cloudcroft were created in the process.

That matters because Cloudcroft was not incidental. It was part of a planned system.

The Sacramento Mountains Museum says that in fall 1898, a survey crew reported not only that a line could be built, but that the area’s beauty would attract visitors. The museum also says the crew suggested the name “Cloudcroft,” glossed as “a pasture for the clouds.” The Village of Cloudcroft uses a similar explanation, saying the name is related to an English description of a clearing covered in clouds. The exact etymology should be treated as a widely repeated origin story rather than a rigorously documented linguistic fact, but the meaning is consistent across local sources: a high clearing associated with clouds.

The town’s founding logic was therefore double: timber below and tourists above.

## 3. Railroad, resort, and mountain town beginnings

The first phase of Cloudcroft’s life tied transportation and tourism together so tightly that one makes little sense without the other.

The National Register historical context document says construction of the mountain line began in 1898. The museum says the railroad reached Toboggan Canyon by the end of that year. In 1899, before the tracks fully reached the summit, John Arthur Eddy opened The Pavilion with a kitchen, dining room, parlor, entertainment hall, and forty tents on wooden platforms. Early visitors came by train as far as Toboggan Canyon and then continued by stagecoach to the summit.

That sequence matters. Cloudcroft became a resort before it became a fully rail-served town.

The rail line reached Cloudcroft in early 1900, according to the Sacramento Mountains Museum, and the depot soon made “meeting the train” a daily village ritual. The museum says three trains arrived daily carrying passengers, mail, and logs. The town thus functioned at once as a pleasure resort and as part of a working railroad-and-logging system.

The railroad itself was remarkable. Historical and technical sources consistently describe it as one of the most dramatic mountain rail lines in the Southwest. The National Register study says the line from Alamogordo into the Sacramento Mountains symbolized a new industrial-commercial phase in the region and transformed the local economy from frontier farming and ranching into something more capital-intensive and connected to outside markets.

The best-known surviving structure from that era is the Mexican Canyon Trestle. Lincoln National Forest says the “Cloud-Climbing Railroad” ran from 1899 to 1947, and the Mexican Canyon Trestle remains one of its most visible surviving landmarks. The National Register nomination and associated summaries describe the trestle as a curved wooden structure 323 feet long and 52 feet high, built in 1899. It endures because most of the rest of the railroad does not.

Rail passenger service ended in 1938, according to the museum, and the final freight train came down in 1947. That end date matters because it marks the close of the first great Cloudcroft era: the town’s life as a rail-linked summit resort and logging gateway.

## 4. The Lodge and the making of Cloudcroft’s identity

If the railroad created Cloudcroft, The Lodge gave it a durable identity.

The current official history page for The Lodge says the original resort development was constructed in 1899 by the Alamogordo and Sacramento Mountain Railway as a by-product of the search for timber and railway ties. It adds that the first building was a pavilion, now remembered through The Pavilion Bed and Breakfast. As the actual log-constructed Lodge reached completion in 1901, period newspaper coverage described it as a lavish but fitting mountain retreat.

The Lodge history page is useful because it shows how quickly Cloudcroft moved from rough summit camp to aspirational resort. By 1908, the site was advertising not just the hotel but a package of leisure: dining, dancing, tennis, golf links, bowling, billiards, burro trips, and children’s play spaces. Cloudcroft was not merely a stop on the line. It was a curated escape.

Then came the fire.

The Lodge’s official history says a disastrous fire on June 13, 1909 destroyed the original Lodge, likely because of a defective flue in the kitchen fireplace. That could have ended the place’s resort future. The same official history says local residents feared exactly that. But a coalition led by Dr. H. E. Stevenson persuaded the railroad to rebuild, in part by arguing for the health benefits of the climate. By 1911, the new Lodge had opened on its current, more scenic site.

That rebuild was more than a construction event. It was a civic decision about what Cloudcroft would be.

The Lodge page says the railroad required supporters to buy shares in the new building to show local commitment. The response, it says, was overwhelming. That suggests Cloudcroft had already become invested in its resort identity, economically and psychologically.

The Lodge’s influence continued beyond architecture. It anchored elite and aspirational tourism, gave the village a social center, and helped define mountain leisure in southern New Mexico. The current property’s historical narrative and local reporting also tie the Lodge to golf, scenic dining, and the broader sense that Cloudcroft should be understood as a high-country retreat, not just a timber outpost.

There are, however, a few historical claims around The Lodge that should be handled carefully. Local and tourism writing sometimes compress the dates of the pavilion, the first Lodge, and the rebuilt Lodge into one smooth story. The official Lodge page is the strongest source for separating those phases: pavilion in 1899, Lodge completed in 1901, fire in 1909, rebuild and reopening in 1911.

## 5. How the town grew and changed

Cloudcroft grew from a resort-and-railroad settlement into a more complete village with schools, churches, businesses, utilities, and government, but it never lost its dependence on visitors.

The business district formed around the rail-served resort economy and later around the automobile town that replaced it. Burro Avenue became the village’s main commercial spine. Local historical and present-day materials show that the town’s physical core remained compact. Cloudcroft did not sprawl into a large mountain city. It stayed small, which preserved some character but also limited its infrastructure and economic base.

Civic institutions followed. The red-brick Cloudcroft School building dates to 1918, according to the Henry Trost Historical Organization, and survives as one of the village’s more visible historic structures. This matters because it signals that Cloudcroft had moved beyond resort dependence into permanent community life, with enough year-round presence to require substantial school facilities.

Municipal government came later than the town’s founding. New Mexico municipal records and a Village of Cloudcroft audit state that Cloudcroft was incorporated in 1948. That is easy to miss if one assumes the town and the village government were born together. They were not. Cloudcroft existed as a place long before it existed as an incorporated municipality.

Water and utilities became defining constraints. Mountain resort imagery can obscure how hard it is to run a village at this elevation in a dry region. A 2007 High Country News report on Cloudcroft’s water-reuse system said the village’s potable reuse effort grew out of necessity: Cloudcroft was high, dry, and dealing with drought. That was not the start of Cloudcroft’s water problems. It was one more chapter in a long structural fact of mountain settlement there. Growth has always had to answer to supply, terrain, and cost.

Forest use also remained central. The National Register railroad-logging study makes clear that commercial logging transformed the region in the railroad era. Over time, that world faded, but forest identity did not. Cloudcroft’s economy moved more heavily toward tourism, second homes, outdoor recreation, and service activity, but the surrounding forest remained both asset and risk.

That risk shows up in fire history. Cloudcroft suffered both landmark fires and ordinary destructive fires that reshaped the village center. KRWG reported that a December 13, 2010 fire destroyed two buildings housing six businesses on Burro Avenue and damaged others with smoke. That was not a founding-era blaze like the Lodge fire, but it fits the same broader pattern: in a mountain town built of combustible materials and tight commercial blocks, fire has repeatedly forced reset and rebuilding.

## 6. Major turning points in Cloudcroft history

Several turning points changed Cloudcroft more than others.

### The 1898–1900 railroad and resort buildout
This is the decisive beginning. Without the railroad survey and the decision to build both for timber extraction and tourist travel, Cloudcroft as we know it would not exist.

### The 1909 Lodge fire and 1911 rebuilding
This may be the most important symbolic moment in town history. The fire could have broken confidence in the resort model. Rebuilding did the opposite. It confirmed that Cloudcroft would continue to define itself through mountain hospitality and leisure.

### The decline of rail and the rise of roads
The museum says passenger service ended in 1938 and freight in 1947. That was a structural change, not just a transportation update. For the first half of Cloudcroft’s existence, the train was central. Once roads and automobiles took over, the town had to become an automobile destination. That changed visitor behavior, supply systems, and the shape of local business.

### Incorporation in 1948
Incorporation marked Cloudcroft’s shift from a place with history to a village with formal civic machinery. That matters for understanding later questions of water, wastewater, roads, planning, public safety, and local politics.

### Late-20th-century preservation and reinvention
Cloudcroft’s built environment survived partly because the town remained small and somewhat out of the way, but survival was not automatic. Local reporting in 2024 noted that there were periods when the Lodge’s golf course nearly gave way to subdivision plans. The implication is larger than golf: the resort-era inheritance was often economically fragile. Cloudcroft endured by repeatedly choosing some version of preservation and reinvention over full erasure.

### Modern fire and infrastructure pressures
The 2010 downtown fire and the 2000s-era water stresses show that the village’s contemporary history is not simply one of charming continuity. It is a story of maintaining a historic mountain community under modern infrastructure strain.

## 7. What from the past still shapes Cloudcroft now

Much of Cloudcroft’s past is still visible if you know what you are looking at.

The most obvious holdovers are physical:
- The Lodge
- The Pavilion
- the old school building
- the compact village core
- the Mexican Canyon Trestle
- the railroad grades that still shape trails, viewpoints, and local memory

But the deeper holdovers are structural.

Cloudcroft still lives on the logic that created it. People go there for altitude, cool weather, scenery, and relief from the basin below. That is the same basic logic the railroad discovered in the 1890s.

The town also still carries the tension between use and preservation. It was founded through resource extraction and tourism at the same time. Today, the extractive side is far weaker, but the tension remains in different form: tourism versus small-town life, second homes versus local needs, preservation versus redevelopment, visitation versus water limits.

Some popular stories should be handled carefully. For example:
- The name “Cloudcroft” is almost always glossed as “pasture for the clouds,” but local sources present that as an origin story, not as a rigorously sourced linguistic finding.
- The golf course is often casually dated to 1899 in tourism or golf writing, but the strongest official Lodge history supports 1899 for the resort project and 1901 for completion of the original Lodge building. The exact opening chronology for golf deserves care.
- Cloudcroft is often described as unchanged. That is only partly true. Its scale is small and many historic elements remain, but rail service vanished, highways reshaped access, buildings burned and were rebuilt, water systems changed, and the economy moved from railroad-and-logging support toward modern tourism and second-home use.

A present-day visitor can still see the bones of the old story:
- the trestle and grades of the Cloud-Climbing Railroad
- the Lodge’s rebuilt presence
- the village’s compact layout
- the ongoing dependence on climate, scenery, and seasonal traffic

That is the most useful historical conclusion. Cloudcroft is not a museum piece. It is a living village whose present still follows the lines laid down by railroad surveyors, resort builders, and a mountain economy that always had to balance beauty with practicality.

## 9. Fact-check notes and open questions

- **Name origin:** “Cloudcroft” as “pasture for the clouds” is widely repeated by local sources, but I did not locate a stronger primary source proving the exact linguistic origin.
- **Golf chronology:** The resort history clearly supports 1899 for the initial resort buildout and 1901 for the original Lodge’s completion. Specific golf-course opening claims are less cleanly documented in the strongest current official source.
- **Exact civic-development sequence:** I found stronger sources for incorporation than for some early public-works milestones. A fuller village-archives review could sharpen the history of water, utilities, and road governance.
- **Pavilion fire dates:** Local sources agree that the Pavilion burned twice and was rebuilt each time, but the exact dates were not as clearly documented in the strongest sources I used here.
- **Population and growth history:** I avoided extended historic population claims because the best current sources I reviewed were stronger on institutions and events than on a long census narrative.
