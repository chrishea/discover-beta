Day Trip: Alameda Park Zoo
A living piece of Southwest history, just down the mountain

Founded in 1898 as a deer park to entertain railroad passengers waiting for their trains to refuel, the Alameda Park Zoo in Alamogordo claims the title of oldest zoo in the Southwestern United States — and at just 35 minutes from Cloudcroft, it makes for an easy and rewarding afternoon escape.

The 12-acre zoo is home to nearly 200 animals representing 90 species, and recent additions have given regulars and first-timers alike fresh reasons to visit. Two Linnaeus's two-toed sloths have joined the collection, and they're drawing plenty of admirers. Here's a tip: the zoo Facebook page announced new Wednesday hours: open until 9:00 p.m., making a mid-week evening visit the ideal time to catch the sloths during their most active hours.

The zoo is also a Species Survival Plan Captive Facility for the Mexican gray wolf — a genuine conservation effort tucked inside what feels like a leisurely neighborhood stroll.

The campus holds an education center, picnic area, playground, and gift shop. Summer camps are available for young animal enthusiasts, and the Eco-Ranger Junior Zookeeper program gives children hands-on experience.
Admission is affordable, parking is free, and the zoo is open Wednesday through Monday from 9:00 a.m. to 5:00 p.m. Check their website for seasonal hours.

Fire Risk and Insurance: What Cloudcroft Homeowners Should Know

Ashley Dalton Agency Owner speaks to changing insurance norms in high risk wildfire zones.

BY ASHLEY DALTON

Living in Cloudcroft and the Sacramento Mountains comes with undeniable beauty — tall pines, cool mountain summers and stunning views.

But the same forests that make our community special also bring a reality we can't ignore: wildfire risk.

In recent years, wildfire exposure has become more than just a safety concern. It has also become a major factor in the insurance market. Many homeowners across New Mexico are seeing higher premiums, stricter policy requirements, and in some cases non-renewal notices.

Understanding why this is happening can help homeowners navigate the changes.

Why Insurance Is Changing

Across New Mexico, insurance companies are reassessing wildfire exposure in ways we haven't seen before. Several factors are driving the shift. Large and destructive wildfires have occurred across the state in recent years. Drought conditions have increased fire danger, and rebuilding costs have risen dramatically since 2020 due to higher prices for materials and labor.

At the same time, insurers now use sophisticated wildfire modeling to evaluate individual properties. These models analyze factors such as vegetation density, terrain, proximity to fire stations, roofing materials, and historical fire behavior in the surrounding area.

When those models identify areas of elevated wildfire risk, insurers typically respond in a few predictable ways: premiums increase, underwriting guidelines tighten, and in some cases companies reduce their exposure in high-risk areas by limiting new policies or choosing not to renew existing ones.

While these decisions are based on risk management, they can have real consequences for homeowners.

Why Cloudcroft Scores High

Wildfire hazard assessments from the New Mexico Energy, Minerals and Natural Resources Department consistently place parts of Otero County — including Cloudcroft — among the highest wildfire-risk areas in the state.
There are several reasons for this rating. Cloudcroft sits in dense, continuous forest fuels.

The terrain is steep in many places, which can accelerate fire spread. Some neighborhoods have limited access roads, and the region experiences seasonal dry lightning.

In addition, many homes are located close to heavy timber.

On statewide wildfire planning maps, the Cloudcroft area often falls into "very high" or "extreme" wildfire hazard categories. Those classifications directly influence how insurance companies evaluate properties here.

What Homeowners Are Seeing

Because of these factors, some homeowners are already experiencing changes in their insurance coverage.

These changes may include higher premiums, roof-age requirements, inspections before policy renewal, or requests for defensible-space improvements. In some cases, companies may issue non-renewal notices based on wildfire exposure.

It is important to understand that most wildfire-related actions involve non-renewals, not cancellations.

A cancellation occurs during the active policy term. A non-renewal happens when the policy period ends and the insurer decides not to continue coverage.

Your Rights

New Mexico law requires insurance companies to follow specific notice rules. Typically, insurers must provide at least 10 days' notice for a cancellation and at least 30 days' notice for a non-renewal.

If you receive a notice, you have the right to request a written explanation and you can contact the New Mexico Office of Superintendent of Insurance for consumer assistance. It is also important to remember that different companies evaluate risk differently, so shopping the market can still be worthwhile.

A Safety Net

For homeowners who cannot obtain insurance through traditional companies, New Mexico operates the FAIR Plan, which serves as the state's insurer of last resort.
Coverage limits have increased in recent years, and more homeowners in high-risk wildfire areas are relying on it. While the coverage may be more basic than standard policies, it ensures that homeowners can still maintain insurance if private carriers withdraw.

What You Can Do

There is some encouraging news. Insurance companies are increasingly paying attention to wildfire-mitigation efforts.

Steps such as maintaining defensible space around the home, thinning vegetation, using fire-resistant roofing, cleaning gutters, and ensuring emergency vehicle access can make a difference. These improvements not only reduce wildfire risk but may also help during insurance underwriting reviews.

Final Thought

Cloudcroft's wildfire-risk rating does not mean homes here are uninsurable. But it does mean the insurance market is tightening, and pricing increasingly reflects wildfire exposure.

Living in the mountains always involves trade-offs. With preparation, good information and community efforts to reduce wildfire risk, homeowners can better protect both their properties and their ability to stay insured.
