# Lincoln National Forest

**More than 1.1 million acres of mountains, canyons, and pine forest surrounding Cloudcroft — with trails, campgrounds, and year-round recreation right outside your door.**

---

## Overview

Cloudcroft sits inside Lincoln National Forest. That's not a metaphor — the village is literally surrounded by national forest land, which means trailheads, campgrounds, and open forest roads start at the edge of town. For visitors, that's a significant advantage. You don't drive to the outdoors here. You step into it.

The Lincoln spans three ranger districts: the Sacramento (which covers Cloudcroft and Alamogordo), the Smokey Bear (centered around Ruidoso and Capitan), and the Guadalupe (near Carlsbad). The Sacramento District is the one most relevant to Cloudcroft visitors. It's the most heavily developed of the three, with 10 developed campgrounds, five group camping sites, more than 50 trails, and several notable day-use areas including the Trestle Recreation Area.

The forest was originally established in 1902 as the Sacramento National Forest and later renamed in honor of Abraham Lincoln. It's also the birthplace of Smokey Bear — the real one, a bear cub rescued from a forest fire in the Capitan Mountains in 1950.

## Getting There

Cloudcroft is the primary gateway to the Sacramento Ranger District. From Alamogordo, take US Highway 82 east approximately 16 miles up the mountain. The drive gains more than 4,700 feet in elevation over roughly 35 miles.

From El Paso: Take US-54 north to Alamogordo, then US-82 east. About 90 miles total.
From Albuquerque: Take I-25 south to Highway 70 east, then connect to US-82. Approximately 3.5 hours.

## Recreation Near Cloudcroft

### Hiking

The Sacramento District has more than 50 trails ranging from short family walks to multi-day backpacking routes. Most are shorter than 3 miles, but several long trails push into serious backcountry.

**Trails near Cloudcroft:**

| Trail | Distance | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Osha Trail | 2.5 miles | Starts north of Cloudcroft on US-82; flat walk through fir forest with basin views |
| Trestle Recreation Area trails | Short loops | Scenic walk with canyon views and historic railroad trestle overlooks |
| Rim Trail (T105) | 29 miles | High-elevation multi-day route; connects to multiple shorter trails |
| Atkinson Field Trail (T111) | 1.6 miles | Connects Rim Trail to Alamo Peak Trail |
| Dog Canyon Trail (T106) | 5.5 miles | Climbs from Oliver Lee State Park to forest elevation; dramatic desert-to-mountain transition |
| Grindstone Lake Loop | 7 miles | Scenic loop around Grindstone Lake; good for families and casual hikers |
| Bluff Springs | Day use | Partially developed area near a scenic waterfall — *currently under temporary closure as of early 2026; verify before visiting* |

**Trail use types:** Most Sacramento District trails allow hiking and horseback riding. Several allow mountain biking. Some allow OHVs and motorcycles. Check individual trail pages at fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln before heading out.

### Camping

The Sacramento District has 10 developed campgrounds and 5 group sites. Three areas — James Canyon, Upper Karr Canyon, and Lower Karr Canyon — are free. Most campgrounds operate on a first-come, first-served basis. Group sites require advance reservations through Recreation.gov.

**Notable campgrounds near Cloudcroft:**

- **Apache Campground** — 25 sites at 8,900 feet in mixed conifer/aspen forest; tables, fire rings, toilets
- **Slide Group Area** — 2 miles from Cloudcroft; accommodates up to 90 people; backs into the Rim Trail; meadow views of the Tularosa Basin; reservation required
- **Aspen Group Campground** — up to 70 people; reservation required; vault toilets
- **Black Bear Group Campground** — up to 70 people; reservation required
- **Upper Karr Canyon** — free; open year-round; popular for dispersed camping and winter snow play (designated sledding/tubing area)
- **James Canyon** — free; generally open May to September
- **Lower Karr Canyon** — free; south of Cloudcroft; popular for dispersed camping

**Dispersed camping** is allowed in many areas — drive up to 300 feet from a public road onto forest land and camp for free, no permit required. No water or restrooms in dispersed areas. Consult the current Motor Vehicle Use Map for eligible zones. A camping stay limit order is currently in effect — verify current rules at fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/alerts.

Reserve campgrounds at [recreation.gov](https://www.recreation.gov/gateways/1100). Book early for peak season (summer weekends fill fast).

### Winter Recreation

Winter turns the Sacramento Mountains into a quieter, snow-covered version of itself. Snowshoeing and cross-country skiing are available on maintained trails near Cloudcroft.

- **Cross-country skiing:** Fir Trail (T122, 1.9 miles) and Little Apache Trail (T124, 2.8 miles) are maintained for skiing in winter, accessible from Silver Overflow Campground
- **Snow sledding and tubing:** Upper Karr Canyon is the designated snow play area near Cloudcroft
- **Downhill skiing:** Ski Cloudcroft is just minutes from the village (8,500–9,500 ft elevation); Ski Apache (operated by the Mescalero Apache Tribe) is near Ruidoso with 750 acres and a 1,900-foot vertical drop
- **Snowshoeing:** Most hiking trails accessible on snowshoes; no permit required

### Other Activities

- **Mountain biking** — Several trails in the Sacramento District allow bikes; the Grindstone Trail System near Ruidoso is considered world-class
- **OHV/ATV riding** — Designated OHV routes throughout the forest; permits required; see fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln for permitted routes
- **Horseback riding** — Most trails open to equestrians
- **Hunting and fishing** — Permitted with appropriate New Mexico state licenses
- **Wildlife viewing** — Elk, deer, black bears, wild turkeys, and a wide variety of birds throughout the forest
- **Stargazing** — Minimal light pollution at elevation; excellent conditions

## Key Day-Use Sites Near Cloudcroft

**Trestle Recreation Area**
On the western edge of Cloudcroft off US-82. Short trails lead to overlooks of the Mexican Canyon Railroad Trestle — a 323-foot-long, 52-foot-high historic wooden trestle built in 1898. Listed on the National Register of Historic Places. No fee for day use.

**Sunspot Scenic Byway (NM-6563)**
A 15.5-mile paved two-lane road running south from Cloudcroft to the village of Sunspot. Spectacular views of the Tularosa Basin along the route. Ends at the National Solar Observatory (Sunspot Solar Observatory), open to the public seasonally.

**Bluff Springs**
A partially developed day-use area near a scenic waterfall. *Currently under temporary closure as of early 2026 — check fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/alerts before visiting.*

## Permits and Passes

Some activities require permits or passes:

- **America the Beautiful / Interagency Annual Pass** — $80/year; covers entrance fees at federal recreation sites; sold at ranger district offices
- **Day-use fees** — Some picnic areas charge a $5 fee
- **Firewood collection** — Permit required; up to 4 cords of dead/down or green standing timber; available at ranger offices
- **Christmas tree cutting** — Annual permits sold at forest headquarters and ranger district offices during the holiday season
- **OHV riding** — Designated routes only; contact ranger office for current permit requirements
- **Group campground reservations** — Book at Recreation.gov

**No fee required** for dispersed camping, most trails, or winter snow play areas.

## Fire Safety

The Lincoln National Forest is a high fire-danger environment, particularly in dry spring and summer conditions. Fire restrictions can be imposed at any time.

As of April 2026: Stage 1 Fire Restrictions are in effect on the Lincoln National Forest. Check current restrictions at fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/alerts before lighting any campfire.

Always check conditions before your visit. Current fire danger status: **High**.

## Practical Tips

- **Water:** Many dispersed areas and free campgrounds have no water. Bring more than you think you'll need.
- **Weather:** Conditions change fast at 9,000 feet. Summer afternoon thunderstorms are common. Check forecasts before heading out.
- **Altitude:** Cloudcroft sits at 8,600–9,000 feet. Give yourself time to adjust, especially if arriving from sea level.
- **Leave No Trace:** Pack out everything you pack in. Camp at least 200 feet from water sources when dispersed camping.
- **Military land:** Some areas adjacent to the forest are part of Holloman AFB and White Sands Missile Range. Stay on mapped public land.
