# Hikes Near Cloudcroft, Ranked Easiest to Hardest

## 1. Grand View Trail (T130)

**Why I like it:**
This is the easy one I'd give almost anybody first. The Forest Service calls it one of its easier hikes, and the trail sheet says the whole Grandview/Salado/Bridal Veil area sits on the old Cloud-Climbing Railroad grade. You get the payoff fast: open views, railroad history, and a lower-elevation feel than the higher Cloudcroft trails. It is a good "first day in the mountains" walk. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails))

**What to know before you go:**
The official Grandview Trails guide says the trailhead is off Forest Road 162C just north of High Rolls, and the trail system there is open year-round. The area guide lists Grand View at 1.3 miles and easy. Even so, it is still mountain country, so expect uneven footing in spots and changing weather. Forest roads can be the bigger issue than the trail itself after storms. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94658))

**Forest Service info / map:**
https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94658

## 2. Pumphouse Ridge Trails (T5661 system)

**Why I like it:**
These are the quietest trails on the list. The Pumphouse Ridge system is a network of short, gentle paths built on old logging roads through ponderosa and mixed conifer forest. Nobody is out here. If you want a peaceful forest walk without steep grades, technical footing, or crowds, this is it. Good for birding, good for clearing your head, good for families with younger kids who need flat ground. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recreation/recarea/?recid=34404&actid=50))

**What to know before you go:**
From Cloudcroft, take NM 130 south 1.2 miles to Apache Canyon Road (FR 24B), turn left, then 0.7 miles to Pumphouse Ridge Road (FR 5661). The individual spur trails range from 0.4 miles (T5661H) to 2.6 miles (T5661F). These are wide, grass-covered former timber roads, not rugged singletrack. Set your expectations accordingly. Open to hiking, horseback riding, and mountain biking. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recreation/bicycling/recarea/?recid=34400&actid=24))

**Forest Service info / map:**
https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recreation/recarea/?recid=34404&actid=50

## 3. Osha Trail (T10)

**Why I like it:**
Osha is the classic Cloudcroft starter trail. The Forest Service literally describes it as "a nice, short hike that's not too hard and close to town," and says it has vistas over the Tularosa Basin and even White Sands. It is the trail I'd choose when I want a solid walk, real scenery, and an easy trailhead without driving deep into the forest. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94558))

**What to know before you go:**
The Forest Service says Osha is 2.2 miles on the current trails list, and the trail guide places it around 8,500 to 8,600 feet. That altitude is the main thing to respect. The guide specifically warns hikers to stop and rest if they feel lightheaded or nauseous, and to carry at least a quart of water, a snack, and an extra jacket. There are no water sources or restrooms at the trailhead. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails))

**Forest Service info / map:**
https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94558

## 4. Salado Canyon Trail (T128)

**Why I like it:**
This one gives you something different from the usual forest loop. It is part of the old railroad route above High Rolls, and the Forest Service's Grandview Trails guide highlights the restored Salado Canyon trestle remains as one of the area's big attractions. It feels like a hike with a story behind it, not just a path through the woods. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94658))

**What to know before you go:**
The Sacramento Ranger District trail map lists Salado Canyon at 1.6 miles and easy, and the Grandview guide says the three-trail area is open year-round. The main caution here is not steepness so much as paying attention around exposed trestle and canyon features and knowing you are hiking in a lower, warmer zone than Cloudcroft proper. The trailhead access is via FR 162C north of High Rolls. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94736))

**Forest Service info / map:**
https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94658

## 5. Bluff Springs / Benson Ridge Trail (T5006)

**Why I like it:**
This is the waterfall hike. A spring-fed cascade drops off a roughly 100-foot plateau into a lush riparian pocket of ferns and wildflowers. It is the signature natural feature of the Cloudcroft backcountry, and the short spur to the falls is only 0.2 miles, making it accessible to almost anyone. The full Benson Ridge trail runs 2.3 miles with moderate elevation change, so you can make it as short or as long as you want. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/null/recarea/?recid=34322&actid=24))

**What to know before you go:**
From Cloudcroft, take NM 130 south 1.9 miles to the Sunspot Highway (NM 6563), turn right, go 8.5 miles to Rio Penasco Road (CR C17), turn left, then 3.7 miles to Bluff Springs. About a 20-minute drive. Important: there has been a Bluff Springs temporary closure order on the Sacramento Ranger District in recent years. Call the ranger district at 575-682-2551 to verify the area is open before driving out. The Taylor Canyon Trail (T5007C, 2.0 miles) provides an alternate route in from County Road C14. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recarea/?recid=40310))

**Forest Service info / map:**
https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/null/recarea/?recid=34322&actid=24

## 6. Cloud-Climbing Trestle Trail (T5001)

**Why I like it:**
This is the best short trail near Cloudcroft if you like railroad history and want something that feels a little more distinctive than a basic out-and-back. The Forest Service says it runs from just south of Trestle Recreation Area to the southern viewing area for the Mexican Canyon Trestle. I like it because the scenery is tied to one of the area's defining landmarks, and it feels very Cloudcroft. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails))

**What to know before you go:**
The current Sacramento trail map lists the Cloud-Climbing Trestle Trail at 1.5 miles and moderate. The general trails page lists it at 1.3 miles, so I would trust the route to be short but not obsess over the exact tenth of a mile. More important: the trails page says the Old Cloudcroft Highway Trail is not currently accessible from Trestle Recreation Area because of area closure issues there, so keep your route simple and check the latest Forest Service status before you go. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94736))

**Forest Service info / map:**
https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails

## 7. Switchback + Old Cloudcroft Highway Loop (T5004 / T5002)

**Why I like it:**
This is the trail you can walk to from town. The Switchback Trail and Old Cloudcroft Highway Trail combine into a roughly 4.4-mile loop that follows the historic Alamogordo & Sacramento Mountain Railway corridor through ponderosa pine forest with views of the Tularosa Basin. No driving to a remote trailhead. No forest road logistics. Just walk to the Trestle Recreation Area and go. You can add Toboggan Canyon Trail (T5003, 1.1 miles) for variation. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recreation/hiking/recarea/?recid=34380&actid=50))

**What to know before you go:**
The Old Cloudcroft Highway Trail (T5002) begins at the southeast corner of Trestle Recreation Area. The Switchback Trail (T5004) has a 175-foot concrete walkway tunnel under US 82 at Bailey Canyon. In winter, that tunnel can be very icy and dangerous. In warmer months, do not enter if water is flowing through it. These are multi-use trails: hiking, horseback riding, mountain biking, and motorcycles. The loop has moderate elevation change between roughly 7,600 and 8,600 feet. This is a good morning hike that takes 1.5 to 3 hours depending on pace. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recreation/ohv/recarea/?recid=34434&actid=24))

**Forest Service info / map:**
https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recreation/hiking/recarea/?recid=34380&actid=50

## 8. Rim Trail — short sections (T105)

**Why I like it:**
The Rim Trail is the signature long trail in the Sacramento Mountains. It runs 31.2 miles in three sections from Cloudcroft south toward Sunspot, and it was the first National Recreation Trail designated in New Mexico. But you do not have to hike the whole thing. There are 12 access points, so you can do a 2- to 4-mile out-and-back from the North Trailhead at the southern edge of Cloudcroft village and get spectacular views of the Tularosa Basin and White Sands through ponderosa pine, fir, and quaking aspen groves at 8,676 feet. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails/rim-trail-t105))

**What to know before you go:**
The North Trailhead is along NM 130 at the southern edge of the village of Cloudcroft. That is the most accessible starting point. Section 1 runs about 11 miles from Cloudcroft to Karr Canyon Road. Section 2 continues about 9.9 miles to the Sunspot Highway. You do not need to commit to any of that. Pick an access point, hike out 1 to 2 miles, turn around, and come back. The trail ranges from 8,350 to 9,500 feet, and wildlife includes mule deer, elk, and a wide range of bird species. Best months are April through November. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails/rim-trail-t105))

**Forest Service info / map:**
https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails/rim-trail-t105

## 9. Wills Canyon Trail (T5008)

**Why I like it:**
Wills Canyon follows an old steam-engine railroad grade through high-elevation forests and meadows. The grades are more forgiving than the Willie White Trail because of the rail-bed routing, but the scenery is just as good. It is best done as the return leg of a Willie White loop: hike up Willie White (T113) on the steeper route, and come back on Wills Canyon's gentler grade. That loop runs about 10.3 miles and is one of the premier day hikes in the Cloudcroft backcountry. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recreation/recarea/?recid=34456&actid=24))

**What to know before you go:**
The trail runs 4.4 miles one-way and is rated moderate. It begins and ends at the Willie White Trail (T113), so access is via the same trailheads near Bluff Springs along County Road C17 and FR 5009. Elevation ranges from about 8,700 to 9,360 feet with roughly 660 feet of gain. There are steep and rocky sections at the start before the trail transitions to the smoother railroad grade. Open to hiking, horseback riding, mountain biking, and motorized use. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recreation/recarea/?recid=34456&actid=24))

**Forest Service info / map:**
https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recreation/recarea/?recid=34456&actid=24

## 10. San Andres Canyon Trail (T125)

**Why I like it:**
This is the one on this list for hikers who want a little more work and less casual strolling. The Forest Service lists it at 2.0 miles, starting from the Rim Trail and ending at Forest Road 90, and the Sacramento Ranger District trail map rates it difficult. I like it because it gets you off the "easy scenic walk" circuit and into a trail that feels more like a real hike than a sightseeing path. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails))

**What to know before you go:**
This is where I'd start paying closer attention to route planning. It is not ranked difficult because it is long. It is difficult because canyon trails tend to be steeper, rougher, and less forgiving underfoot than the railroad-grade hikes. Also, because it ties into the Rim Trail system, it pays to know exactly where you are starting and where you intend to come out. This is not the hike I'd hand to someone on their first afternoon at 8,676 feet. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails))

**Forest Service info / map:**
https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails

## 11. Willie White Trail (T113)

**Why I like it:**
This is the trail that gives you a bit of everything: forest, meadows, ridgeline views of White Sands and the surrounding mountains, and a small waterfall. The trail runs 5.2 miles one way and climbs about 1,360 feet from the lower trailhead near Bluff Springs to the upper ridgeline. About 90 percent of the surface is rock, so it feels like real backcountry. Combined with Wills Canyon (T5008) for the return, it makes the premier loop hike in the area at roughly 10.3 miles. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recreation/recarea/?recid=34454&actid=24))

**What to know before you go:**
The lower trailhead begins near Bluff Springs along County Road C17 (Rio Penasco Road). A 0.5-mile spur trail (T112) connects to the main trail near the springs. The first 4 miles are mostly uphill with some very technical rocky sections. Elevation ranges from about 8,000 to 9,360 feet. Open to hiking, horseback riding, mountain biking, and OHVs under 50 inches. Best months are April through October. This is an all-day hike if you do the full out-and-back or the loop with Wills Canyon. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recreation/recarea/?recid=34454&actid=24))

**Forest Service info / map:**
https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recreation/recarea/?recid=34454&actid=24

## 12. Dog Canyon Trail (T106) at Oliver Lee Memorial State Park

**Why I like it:**
This is the hardest and most dramatic hike on this list. Dog Canyon cuts deeply into the western escarpment of the Sacramento Mountains, climbing roughly 3,100 to 3,300 feet from the desert floor to Joplin Ridge. You start in Chihuahuan Desert scrub and finish in mountain forest. Along the way, year-round springs support ferns, mossy rocks, and a riparian oasis. The views of the Tularosa Basin, the Organ Mountains, and the full sweep of the desert floor are some of the best in southern New Mexico. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recarea/?recid=34340))

**What to know before you go:**
The trailhead is at Oliver Lee Memorial State Park, about 14 miles south of Alamogordo on Dog Canyon Road (US 54 south to Dog Canyon Road). Driving time from Cloudcroft is 30 to 35 minutes down the mountain via US 82. State park day-use fee required. The trail is 5.5 miles one way, 11 miles round trip, and very steep in places with 25- to 45-percent grades. Plan 8 to 10 hours for the full round trip. Carry plenty of water. Lower sections are exposed with limited shade. If you want the springs and canyon scenery without the full climb, the lower nature trail near the visitor center is an easy alternative. ([emnrd.nm.gov](https://www.emnrd.nm.gov/spd/find-a-park/oliver-lee-memorial-state-park/))

**Forest Service info / map:**
https://www.fs.usda.gov/recarea/lincoln/recarea/?recid=34340

## Quick advice before you go

Cloudcroft hiking is high-country hiking. Osha alone sits around 8,500 to 8,600 feet, and even easy trails can feel harder if you came up from Alamogordo the same day. Weather swings fast. Carry more water than you think you need, and keep an extra layer in the car or pack. Cell service can be spotty once you get away from town. Stay on the trail, leash dogs where required, and check Lincoln National Forest alerts before you leave. As of March 27, 2026, the forest has Stage 1 fire restrictions in effect, and closures or seasonal access limits can change trail logistics. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94558))

These twelve trails give you a real spread of experiences: basin views, old railroad grades, trestle history, waterfall scenery, quiet forest roads, premier loop hikes, and one serious canyon ascent from the desert floor to the mountain top.
