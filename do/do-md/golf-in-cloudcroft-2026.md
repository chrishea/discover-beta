# Where to Play Golf in Cloudcroft (2026)

## 1. Executive summary

If you want to play golf in Cloudcroft, The Lodge Golf Course is the main game that matters.

That is not because it is the longest or most polished course in New Mexico. It is because it is unusually tied to place. You are playing at roughly 9,000 feet, next to one of the region’s landmark hotels, on a historic short course that uses a Scottish-style two-loop setup to turn nine holes into an 18-hole experience. Current official and tourism sources consistently position it as one of the highest courses in New Mexico and one of the core on-site experiences at The Lodge.

For the right player, that is the draw. You are not coming here for a sprawling modern resort layout. You are coming for altitude, history, mountain air, compact shot values, and the fact that a round here fits naturally into a Cloudcroft weekend built around the hotel, dinner, drinks, hiking, and cool weather.

## 3. What the golf experience appears to be

This looks like a short, high-altitude mountain course where setting matters as much as score.

The Lodge’s own site leans hard on views, demanding terrain, elk sightings, and the fact that you are golfing at 9,000 feet. New Mexico Tourism and golf-directory sources reinforce the same picture: this is a compact historic course where the routing and elevation are the story. The course is not sold as a championship-length test. It is sold as a distinctive round in unusual terrain.

The most useful way to think about it is this: it appears to be a destination golf experience for travelers who like quirky, scenic, shot-making courses and do not need a full-scale modern golf complex to have a good time.

It is likely best for:
- golfers who enjoy unusual settings
- players who appreciate short-course strategy
- couples or friends building a full Lodge weekend
- travelers who care about atmosphere and history
- golfers comfortable adjusting expectations away from big-resort polish

It is less obviously built for:
- players seeking long-yardage challenge
- golfers who want a full range-and-practice campus
- golfers who want highly predictable modern resort conditions every day of the season

## 4. Course layout, setting, and playing character

The most consistent description across current and legacy sources is that this is a **Scottish-style 9-hole course played twice for 18 with different tees and flag placements**.

That matters because the golf experience is shaped by repetition with variation rather than by 18 separate holes. New Mexico Tourism says the course is “governed by the Scottish tradition of playing different tees and separate flags on each hole.” GolfPass and other course-detail pages describe the same thing in plainer terms: it is a 9-hole course you play twice with different tee and hole locations.

Verified or strongly supported character points:
- **High elevation:** about 9,000 feet
- **Short course:** the common 9-hole number is about 2,387 yards, par 34
- **18-hole logic:** two-loop format with alternate tees / flags
- **Terrain:** mountain terrain with drop-offs and elevation change
- **Scenery:** long views and frequent wildlife mentions
- **Course type:** resort / daily-fee course, but in a compact and historic mold rather than a sprawling one

Useful playing implication:
- The course likely rewards distance control, trajectory management, and comfort with uneven mountain terrain more than brute force.
- At this elevation, the ball should fly farther than many visitors expect.
- Because the course is short, the challenge is probably more about placement, slopes, wind, and elevation than raw length.

One specific course-detail source says the opening tee shot features about a 150-foot vertical drop. That is not on the current official Lodge site, so I would treat it as a well-circulated supporting detail rather than a primary-source fact. Still, it fits the broader consensus: this is not flat, parkland golf.

## 5. 2026 season and current operations

The clearest current 2026 operational signal is from The Lodge’s Facebook presence, which says the course is officially open for the 2026 season and advertises discounted rates for the first opening weeks, along with a **20% local discount all summer long**.

That tells you several useful things:
1. The course is open now for 2026.
2. The course operates on a seasonal model.
3. The Lodge is actively trying to drive local and early-season play.

The strongest season-end clue comes from two places:
- a current golf-specific Facebook page says the course is open “until October 31st”
- Cloudcroft Reader reported in October 2024 that the course **typically closes on Halloween**, though it stayed open a bit longer that year because of warm weather and steady traffic

That suggests the usual rule of thumb is **spring through late October**, with weather-driven flexibility.

That is one of the course’s practical strengths. A round here does not end in a parking lot. It folds into the larger Lodge experience.

## 6. History and significance

The course matters because The Lodge matters.

The Lodge itself dates to **1899**, according to the current official hotel history page and multiple tourism sources. The original lodge burned in 1909 and the current building was rebuilt and relocated in 1911. The golf course is repeatedly presented as part of that broader historic resort identity.

Historical significance that is strongly supported:
- The Lodge is one of New Mexico’s landmark resort hotels.
- The golf course has long been part of the property’s identity.
- Current tourism and golf sources describe the course as one of the highest in New Mexico, and some say one of the highest in North America.
- The course’s survival has not always been guaranteed. Cloudcroft Reader reported in 2024 that there were years when the course was nearly subdivided into cabin lots during harder times for the property.

That last point is useful because it explains why golf here still feels tied to preservation as much as recreation. The course is not just an amenity. It is part of what kept the Lodge recognizable as the Lodge.

There is also a recurring historical claim in golf directories and tourism blurbs that the course opened in **1899**. Because current official Lodge pages I could verify do not clearly state the golf course’s own opening year separate from the hotel’s founding year, I would treat that exact golf-opening-date claim carefully unless confirmed directly by Lodge staff or a strong historical source dedicated to the course.

## 7. What golfers should know before they go

### Altitude changes the round
At roughly 9,000 feet, your ball will travel farther. That can be fun, but it also changes club selection fast. If you came up from Alamogordo or El Paso the same day, the walk and the swing can both feel different.

### Weather is not a small variable
Cloudcroft summer weather is usually cooler than the basin below, which is part of the appeal. But mountain weather can turn quickly. Layers matter. Afternoon storms are not a theoretical concern in summer mountain golf.

### This is probably more fun if you accept it on its own terms
Do not book it expecting a long modern desert resort course. Book it if you want a scenic, compact, historic mountain round where novelty and place matter.

### Book with a little caution
Because rates, hours, and the exact season window are not fully spelled out on the current official pages I could verify, it is smart to call or use the live tee-time system before building a trip around one exact slot.

### Walk or cart?
Walking appears allowed, but the terrain is mountain terrain, not a flat muni. If you are arriving at altitude, playing 18, and also doing other Cloudcroft activities that day, a cart may make the day easier.

### Make it part of a Lodge weekend
This course makes the most sense when paired with the rest of the Lodge:
- stay overnight
- play in the morning or midday
- take a break
- have a drink or dinner on site

That is where the course’s value is clearest.
