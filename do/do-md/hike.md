# Hikes Near Cloudcroft, Ranked Easiest to Hardest

This ranking is a practical guide, not an official Forest Service difficulty chart. In a few cases, current official sources confirm trail length and allowed uses more clearly than they confirm a precise difficulty rating. Where that is true, this guide says so.

## 1. Osha Trail (T10)

**Why it belongs near the top:**  
Osha is the most reliable “start here” hike near Cloudcroft. The Forest Service describes it as a short hike close to town with views over the Tularosa Basin and White Sands. The current Forest Service trail guide lists it at **2.1 miles** and around **8,600 feet**.

**What to know before you go:**  
The main trailhead is just west of Cloudcroft near the junction of **US 82 and Muchachita Street**. The trail is open year-round and used by hikers, pets, and pack animals. Pets should be leashed. There are **no water sources or restrooms**. Even though this is one of the easier hikes, the elevation matters. Carry water, a snack, and an extra layer.

## 2. Grand View Trail (T130)

**Why it belongs early on the list:**  
Grand View is a strong first-day option because it is part of the **Grandview / Salado / Bridal Veil** trail area above High Rolls and follows the old Cloud-Climbing Railroad grade, which usually means gentler walking than many canyon trails. It offers quick scenery and some railroad history.

## 7. Bluff Springs / Benson Ridge area (T5006)

**Why it belongs here:**  
Bluff Springs remains one of the most attractive short-to-moderate outings in the Cloudcroft backcountry because it offers riparian scenery and access to a well-known springs area.

**What to know before you go:**  
This section needed the most updating. There is a current Forest Service **closure order** in effect from **January 1, 2026 through December 31, 2026** covering the **Bluff Springs recreation site parking lot, vault toilets, and the T112 bridge**. The Forest Service says **alternate day-use access remains open** via **Willie White Trail T113**, which runs west into **T112** and reaches the springs from that side. Separate restrictions also remain in place for **dispersed camping and off-road motor vehicle use** in the broader closure area through **August 31, 2026**.

That means this is no longer a simple drive-to-the-parking-lot destination in the way many older descriptions imply.

## 8. Rim Trail — short sections (T105)

**Why it belongs here:**  
The **Rim Trail** is still one of the defining hikes of the Sacramento Mountains, but for many readers it works best as a **short section hike**, not a through-hike. The current Forest Service page lists it at **31.2 miles**, beginning at the southern end of Cloudcroft along **NM 130** and ending at **Sacramento River Road south of Sunspot**.

**What to know before you go:**  
The best practical advice is simple: start from the north end near Cloudcroft and do an out-and-back. The Forest Service says there are **multiple trailheads**, which supports that approach. The first **0.73 miles** to Slide Campground is **non-motorized**. South of Slide Campground, the trail is open to **motorcycles**.

## 11. Willie White Trail (T113)

**Why it belongs near the top end of difficulty:**  
This is one of the area’s stronger backcountry day hikes, but older descriptions overstated the length. The current Forest Service page lists **Willie White Trail T113 at 4.6 miles**. It begins just west of Bluff Springs along **County Road C17 (Rio Penasco Road)** and ends at **Forest Service Road 5009**.

## 12. Dog Canyon Trail (T106) at Oliver Lee Memorial State Park

**Why it belongs last:**  
This is the hardest hike on the list. New Mexico State Parks describes **Dog Canyon Trail** as a **difficult** trail that begins next to the visitor center, reaches the Lincoln National Forest in **half a mile**, then climbs **5 miles** from **4,400 feet** to **7,600 feet**, ending at **Forest Road 90B**.

**What to know before you go:**  
This trail starts much lower and hotter than the Cloudcroft trails. That matters. ## Quick advice before you go

Cloudcroft hiking is still **high-country hiking**. Even the easier options sit at meaningful elevation. Carry more water than you think you need, and bring an extra layer. The biggest current planning issue is not abstract mountain advice. It is **access and closures**. In this part of the forest, **Bluff Springs access is materially affected by the 2026 closure order**, and **Trestle Recreation Area** has a current **seasonal closure** even though overlook trails remain reachable from the **Village Spur Trail** parking area.

I would also tell readers to check the **Lincoln National Forest alerts page** before leaving town. I confirmed current Bluff Springs closure material there.
