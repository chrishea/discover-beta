# Hikes Near Cloudcroft, Ranked Easiest to Hardest

## 1. Grand View Trail (T130)

**Why I like it:**  
This is the easy one I’d give almost anybody first. The Forest Service calls it one of its easier hikes, and the trail sheet says the whole Grandview/Salado/Bridal Veil area sits on the old Cloud-Climbing Railroad grade. You get the payoff fast: open views, railroad history, and a lower-elevation feel than the higher Cloudcroft trails. It is a good “first day in the mountains” walk. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails))

**What to know before you go:**  
The official Grandview Trails guide says the trailhead is off Forest Road 162C just north of High Rolls, and the trail system there is open year-round. The area guide lists Grand View at 1.3 miles and easy. Even so, it is still mountain country, so expect uneven footing in spots and changing weather. Forest roads can be the bigger issue than the trail itself after storms. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94658))

**Forest Service info / map:**  
https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94658

## 2. Osha Trail (T10)

**Why I like it:**  
Osha is the classic Cloudcroft starter trail. The Forest Service literally describes it as “a nice, short hike that’s not too hard and close to town,” and says it has vistas over the Tularosa Basin and even White Sands. It is the trail I’d choose when I want a solid walk, real scenery, and an easy trailhead without driving deep into the forest. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94558))

**What to know before you go:**  
The Forest Service says Osha is 2.2 miles on the current trails list, and the trail guide places it around 8,500 to 8,600 feet. That altitude is the main thing to respect. The guide specifically warns hikers to stop and rest if they feel lightheaded or nauseous, and to carry at least a quart of water, a snack, and an extra jacket. There are no water sources or restrooms at the trailhead. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails))

**Forest Service info / map:**  
https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94558

## 3. Salado Canyon Trail (T128)

**Why I like it:**  
This one gives you something different from the usual forest loop. It is part of the old railroad route above High Rolls, and the Forest Service’s Grandview Trails guide highlights the restored Salado Canyon trestle remains as one of the area’s big attractions. It feels like a hike with a story behind it, not just a path through the woods. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94658))

**What to know before you go:**  
The Sacramento Ranger District trail map lists Salado Canyon at 1.6 miles and easy, and the Grandview guide says the three-trail area is open year-round. The main caution here is not steepness so much as paying attention around exposed trestle and canyon features and knowing you are hiking in a lower, warmer zone than Cloudcroft proper. The trailhead access is via FR 162C north of High Rolls. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94736))

**Forest Service info / map:**  
https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94658

## 4. Cloud-Climbing Trestle Trail (T5001)

**Why I like it:**  
This is the best short trail near Cloudcroft if you like railroad history and want something that feels a little more distinctive than a basic out-and-back. The Forest Service says it runs from just south of Trestle Recreation Area to the southern viewing area for the Mexican Canyon Trestle. I like it because the scenery is tied to one of the area’s defining landmarks, and it feels very Cloudcroft. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails))

**What to know before you go:**  
The current Sacramento trail map lists the Cloud-Climbing Trestle Trail at 1.5 miles and moderate. The general trails page lists it at 1.3 miles, so I would trust the route to be short but not obsess over the exact tenth of a mile. More important: the trails page says the Old Cloudcroft Highway Trail is not currently accessible from Trestle Recreation Area because of area closure issues there, so keep your route simple and check the latest Forest Service status before you go. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94736))

**Forest Service info / map:**  
https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails

## 5. San Andres Canyon Trail (T125)

**Why I like it:**  
This is the one on this list for hikers who want a little more work and less casual strolling. The Forest Service lists it at 2.0 miles, starting from the Rim Trail and ending at Forest Road 90, and the Sacramento Ranger District trail map rates it difficult. I like it because it gets you off the “easy scenic walk” circuit and into a trail that feels more like a real hike than a sightseeing path. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

**What to know before you go:**  
This is where I’d start paying closer attention to route planning. It is not ranked difficult because it is long. It is difficult because canyon trails tend to be steeper, rougher, and less forgiving underfoot than the railroad-grade hikes. Also, because it ties into the Rim Trail system, it pays to know exactly where you are starting and where you intend to come out. This is not the hike I’d hand to someone on their first afternoon at 9,000 feet. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails?utm_source=chatgpt.com))

**Forest Service info / map:**  
https://www.fs.usda.gov/r03/lincoln/recreation/trails

## Quick advice before you go

Cloudcroft hiking is high-country hiking. Osha alone sits around 8,500 to 8,600 feet, and even easy trails can feel harder if you came up from Alamogordo the same day. Weather swings fast. Carry more water than you think you need, and keep an extra layer in the car or pack. Cell service can be spotty once you get away from town. Stay on the trail, leash dogs where required, and check Lincoln National Forest alerts before you leave. As of March 27, 2026, the forest has Stage 1 fire restrictions in effect, and closures or seasonal access limits can change trail logistics. ([fs.usda.gov](https://www.fs.usda.gov/media/94558))

High-level reasoning: I picked five trails that are close to Cloudcroft, different from one another, and supported by current Forest Service material. I leaned toward hikes that give you a real spread of experiences: basin views, old railroad grades, trestle history, and one harder canyon option.
