About

High-Country Hiking

Cloudcroft hiking is high-country hiking. The trails here sit between 8,500 and 9,000+ feet in the Sacramento Mountains, inside the Lincoln National Forest. That means cooler air, mountain forest, and views that stretch across the Tularosa Basin to White Sands — but it also means altitude changes everything.

Even easy trails can feel harder if you drove up from Alamogordo the same day. Weather swings fast. Carry more water than you think you need, keep an extra layer in the car or pack, and check Lincoln National Forest alerts before you leave. As of March 27, 2026, Stage 1 fire restrictions are in effect forest-wide.

These five trails are close to Cloudcroft, different from one another, and all supported by current Forest Service material — a real spread of experiences from basin views and old railroad grades to trestle history and a harder canyon descent.

The Trails

Easiest to Hardest

Five hikes ranked by difficulty. All are close to Cloudcroft and free to access.

1. Grand View Trail

The easy one for almost anybody. Old railroad grade with open views and a lower-elevation feel.

  • 1.3 miles — Easy
  • Trail T130, off FR 162C north of High Rolls
  • Open year-round
  • Railroad history, basin views
  • Forest Service map

2. Osha Trail

The classic Cloudcroft starter. Close to town, solid scenery, vistas over the Tularosa Basin and White Sands.

  • 2.2 miles — Moderate
  • Trail T10, ~8,500–8,600 ft
  • No water or restrooms at trailhead
  • Carry water, snack, extra jacket
  • Forest Service map

3. Salado Canyon Trail

Railroad route with restored trestle remains. A hike with a story, not just a path through the woods.

  • 1.6 miles — Easy
  • Trail T128, off FR 162C north of High Rolls
  • Open year-round
  • Lower, warmer zone than Cloudcroft proper
  • Forest Service map

4. Cloud-Climbing Trestle Trail

Best short trail near Cloudcroft for railroad history. Ends at the Mexican Canyon Trestle overlook.

  • 1.3–1.5 miles — Moderate
  • Trail T5001, near Trestle Recreation Area
  • Check Forest Service for closure updates
  • Iconic Cloudcroft landmark at the end
  • Forest Service trails page

5. San Andres Canyon Trail

The harder option. Steeper, rougher canyon terrain that ties into the Rim Trail system.

  • 2.0 miles — Difficult
  • Trail T125, starts from Rim Trail
  • Steeper and less forgiving underfoot
  • Know your route before you start
  • Forest Service trails page
⚠️

Altitude changes everything. Even easy trails feel harder at 8,500–9,000 feet. Carry at least a quart of water per person, bring a snack and extra layer, and back off your pace if you arrived from lower elevation the same day. The Forest Service specifically warns hikers to stop and rest if lightheaded or nauseous.

Railroad History

Three of these five trails follow or connect to the old Cloud-Climbing Railroad grade. Grand View, Salado Canyon, and the Cloud-Climbing Trestle Trail all tie into the railroad story that shaped Cloudcroft.

🌎

Basin Views

Osha Trail and the Rim Trail system offer panoramic views across the Tularosa Basin to White Sands. On clear days, the vista stretches over 50 miles from the mountain edge.

🎯

Real Range of Difficulty

From a 1.3-mile railroad-grade stroll to a difficult canyon descent, these five trails cover the full spectrum. First-time visitors start with Grand View or Osha; experienced hikers head for San Andres Canyon.

Good to Know

Stage 1 fire restrictions in effect through Sept 30, 2026. No trailhead fees or permits required. Cell service is spotty on forest trails. Leash dogs where required. Afternoon thunderstorms are common June–August — plan for mornings. Check forest alerts before you go.
