# The Cabins at Cloudcroft: Booking Guide

## 1. Executive summary

The Cabins at Cloudcroft is a small, owner-operated cluster of vintage knotty-pine cabins on Coyote Avenue, within walking distance of Burro Street. Cabins date to 1965 and have been operated under current ownership since 2020 by Karl and Laura Campbell. It is not a resort. It works best for travelers who want a cabin-style stay with a fireplace, a real kitchen, and a quiet mountain setting rather than hotel services.

The strongest current evidence points to roughly 15 to 16 individually different cabins ranging from one-bedroom units up to a four-bedroom/two-bath cabin, all with wood-burning fireplaces or wood stoves, equipped kitchens, flat-screen TVs, free Wi-Fi, and the original 1960s knotty pine interiors. An outdoor pavilion with a karaoke system, Adirondack seating, and regular elk and deer sightings on the property are repeatedly mentioned across sources. Pets are allowed with restrictions. This is a cabin experience, not a hotel experience — expect charm and some variation between units, not standardized rooms.

## 3. What the property appears to be

The Cabins at Cloudcroft is a small, owner-run cabin property — not a hotel, not a resort, and not a vacation-rental aggregator. The owners live on-site or nearby and answer the phone themselves. The cabins are individually different in size, layout, and bed count, but share a clear common identity: 1960s knotty-pine interiors, real fireplaces, full kitchens, and a quiet pine-forest setting.

Guest feedback and ownership commentary both frame this as a "step back in time" experience — original cabinets, vintage charm, and an emphasis on hospitality rather than amenities. The owners are described as hands-on and attentive, and cleanliness comes up repeatedly in reviews.

It looks best for:
- couples and families who want a cabin rather than a hotel room
- travelers who value a real kitchen and a real fireplace
- groups needing multi-bedroom configurations (up to four bedrooms in Cabin 16)
- dog owners with small pets, within the property's limits
- visitors who want to walk to Burro Street shops and restaurants

It looks less ideal for:
- travelers who want resort amenities like a pool, spa, or on-site dining
- guests who need uniform, chain-style standards across units
- parties with large dogs (over 25 pounds, per third-party listings)
- travelers who need modern, minimalist decor

## 5. Food and provisions

There is no on-site restaurant. The value proposition is the opposite: full kitchens in every cabin. Pack groceries or use Cloudcroft's village shops and restaurants on Burro Street, which is a short walk away.

Kitchen equipment consistently referenced:
- small gas range
- microwave
- refrigerator
- coffee pot
- basic cookware (reported in guest reviews; item-by-item inventory should be confirmed)

Outdoor food and gathering:
- BBQ and grill use under the pavilion is referenced across listings
- the pavilion also houses a karaoke system and is used for group gatherings

Because this is a self-catering property, travelers should plan a grocery stop before arrival. The nearest substantial grocery is in Alamogordo (about 20 miles down the mountain); in-village options in Cloudcroft are smaller. Confirm current village grocery options directly or with the owners.

## 6. Grounds and setting

The property sits on Coyote Avenue among tall pines. Guests repeatedly mention elk and deer on the grounds, including bull elk bugling during the fall rut — a genuine draw for wildlife watchers. Adirondack chairs and the outdoor pavilion give guests a place to gather beyond the individual cabins.

Setting strengths:
- walkable to Burro Street (main street) shops and restaurants
- within easy driving distance of Lincoln National Forest trails, Trestle Recreation Area, and Ski Cloudcroft
- quiet pine-forest atmosphere at 8,676 feet
- wildlife sightings on property, not just nearby

What the setting is not:
- lakeside or canyon-view
- a destination resort with its own recreational amenities
- isolated — the village is immediately accessible, which is a plus for most travelers and a minor trade-off for those seeking deep seclusion

What matters for booking is the current state: owner-operated, actively maintained, and visibly preserved in terms of the original 1960s character.

Overall pattern: guests who want a real cabin with character and hands-on hospitality come away pleased. Guests expecting hotel-style uniformity are the ones most likely to be surprised.

## 9. What travelers should verify before booking

Ask directly about:
- which specific cabin you are getting and its bed configuration
- whether your cabin has a wood-burning fireplace or a wood stove
- whether your cabin includes full cookware and dishware or just appliances
- whether your cabin is pet-friendly and the current pet fee and weight limit
- current check-in and check-out times and whether early check-in is available
- whether Wi-Fi extends reliably inside your specific cabin
- whether AC, a hot tub, or any other unit-specific amenity is included
- current nightly rate, minimum-stay requirements, and seasonal pricing
- cancellation and damage policies
- whether the pavilion/karaoke is available on a scheduled or first-come basis
- accessibility features if needed (most 1965-era cabins have steps; confirm ground-floor access)
